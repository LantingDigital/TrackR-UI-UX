Three major Southern California theme parks are building new coasters at the same time. This has not happened in this market in years, and the timing is not a coincidence. The 2028 LA Olympics is coming, bringing an estimated $13 to $18 billion in economic impact to the six-county region, and every park in the area wants a new headline attraction open before the world shows up.

Here is what each park is building, where they stand, and how it all fits together.

## Six Flags Magic Mountain: The World's First Vekoma Thrill Glider

Blue track for an unannounced 19th coaster arrived at Magic Mountain earlier in 2026, and the park teased that "the skyline won't be the same." Screamscape has been tracking this project closely and reports it as a Vekoma Thrill Glider, a ride concept that has never been built before.

**What is a Thrill Glider?** Based on permit filings (labeled "Flags Thr Glider Coaster"), enthusiast analysis, and Screamscape's reporting, it is an inverted straddle-seating motorbike-style launch coaster. Think of it as an under-track, launch-powered cousin to TRON but with inversions. Riders straddle the vehicle in a motorbike position, hanging below the track, and get launched through inversions and high-speed elements.

Vekoma does not yet list a "Thrill Glider" on their public product pages, which confirms this is a prototype variant rather than a catalog model. Magic Mountain would be getting the first installation of a ride type that does not exist anywhere else in the world. That is a significant marketing coup for a park that has built its identity around having the most coasters.

**The Ninja connection.** Screamscape has repeatedly suggested that the Thrill Glider functionally replaces Ninja, the aging Arrow suspended coaster that has dealt with earthquake damage, tree losses, and general wear. The project area includes the Golden Bear Theater, which has been cleared, and potentially the Ninja footprint. Forum discussions on ParkFans and CoasterForce have associated the project with the working name "Cyber Cycle," though neither the name nor Ninja's closure are confirmed by the park.

**Timeline.** This one is murky. The park's own teaser post leans toward a 2027 opening window, and Screamscape openly says they do not know if it will open in mid/late 2026 or slip to early 2027. Corporate apparently wavered on the Thrill Glider concept at one point and briefly considered a different Vekoma launched coaster, which is why timelines have slipped.

The bottom line: Magic Mountain is getting a world-first prototype coaster that would make it the 19th coaster at the park, reinforcing its position as the park with the most coasters in North America. But the opening date is still uncertain.

## Knott's Berry Farm: MonteZOOMa: The Forbidden Fortress

Knott's is rebuilding one of its most iconic rides. Montezooma's Revenge, the classic Schwarzkopf shuttle loop that operated from 1978 to 2019, is being reconstructed as MonteZOOMa: The Forbidden Fortress with a new story, updated hardware, and a redesigned queue.

Track and supports have been going up, with construction videos showing the rebuilt loop and launch area progressing. As of March 2026, workers are finishing track installation and station/queue work. Electrical and LSM infrastructure (rebar, equipment pads) and a reconfigured entrance area are visible from the park.

This is not a new layout. It is a modernization of the existing shuttle loop concept with new theming, a new launch system (LSM instead of the original flywheel), and updated safety systems. But it gives Knott's a refreshed launch-focused headliner at a time when both Universal and Magic Mountain are bringing in new hardware.

The opening target is "hopefully 2026," but even enthusiasts following the construction closely are not confident enough to call an exact month. The rebuild has taken longer than expected, and the park has not committed to a specific date.

## Universal Studios Hollywood: Fast & Furious: Hollywood Drift

This is the most locked-in of the three projects. Universal has fully announced Fast & Furious: Hollywood Drift as its first high-speed outdoor coaster, opening summer 2026 on the Upper Lot.

**The specs are serious:** up to 72 mph, approximately 4,100 feet of elevated track, multiple inversions, and ride vehicles that spin 360 degrees to simulate high-speed drifting. The coaster threads over the park, including over segments of the multi-level Starway escalators, and replaces the old special effects/animal show area.

This is not a typical themed coaster. The 360-degree spinning vehicles add a dimension that traditional coasters cannot replicate. At 72 mph with inversions and spinning, Hollywood Drift will be the fastest coaster in the Universal Destinations lineup when it opens.

The themed garage-style queue ties into the Fast & Furious franchise, which gives Universal an IP hook that Magic Mountain and Knott's do not have. Universal is betting on both speed and franchise recognition to win the coverage war.

Construction is well advanced, with the track clearly visible from multiple points in the park. Summer 2026 is a firm target that Universal has publicly committed to.

## The Olympics Factor

The 2028 LA Olympics is the accelerant behind all of this spending. The Southern California Association of Governments estimates the Games will generate $13 to $18 billion in economic impact, with $8.9 to $12 billion of that in Los Angeles County alone. Millions of domestic and international visitors will be in the area, and a significant percentage of them will extend their trips to include theme park visits.

The LA28 plan is explicitly "no-build" for permanent venues, meaning the Olympics are not directly funding rides. But the tourism surge is real, and every major park in the area wants new attractions open and marketable by the time the world arrives. That timeline aligns perfectly with Universal targeting 2026, Knott's aiming for 2026, and Magic Mountain sliding its prototype Vekoma piece to the 2026-2027 window.

The merged Six Flags/Cedar Fair entity now controls both Magic Mountain and Knott's, which adds an interesting strategic dimension. How do you time marquee projects at two parks you own in the same market without cannibalizing your own attendance? The answer appears to be: spend at both, but give them different identities. Magic Mountain gets the world-first prototype. Knott's gets the legacy revival. Different appeal, same market, same corporate parent.

## What This Means for Enthusiasts

If you are planning a Southern California coaster trip in 2026 or 2027, the timing could not be better. Within a roughly 60-mile radius, three major parks will each have a new or significantly reimagined headliner.

Magic Mountain already has 18 coasters and will add a 19th that is unlike anything else in the world. Knott's is refreshing a legacy ride that has been missed since 2019. Universal is adding a genuine thrill coaster to a park that has traditionally been about dark rides and shows.

The competition benefits riders. When parks are racing to outdo each other, they spend more, they innovate more, and they market harder. Southern California was already one of the best regions in the country for roller coasters. Over the next two years, it is going to get even better.
