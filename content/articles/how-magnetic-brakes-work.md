Every modern roller coaster uses magnetic brakes. They have no friction pads, no hydraulic lines, no moving parts that wear out. They work in rain, heat, cold, and dust. They cannot fail in a way that results in no braking (the physics makes that impossible). And they changed the coaster industry by making high-speed rides dramatically safer and cheaper to maintain.

But how do magnets slow down a multi-ton train moving at 70 mph? The answer involves a physics concept called eddy currents, and once you understand it, you will notice magnetic brakes on every coaster you ride.

## The Old Way: Friction Brakes

Before magnetic brakes, roller coasters used friction brakes. These worked exactly like car brakes: a pad pressed against a metal fin on the underside of the train, and friction slowed the train down. Simple. Reliable. And full of problems.

Friction brakes wear out. The pads need regular replacement. They generate heat, which changes their performance. In wet conditions, friction drops and braking distance increases (just like driving on wet roads). In cold weather, the pads can seize. And because they are mechanical systems with moving parts (actuators, pneumatic cylinders, springs), they require ongoing maintenance.

More critically, friction brakes can fail in the open position. If the actuator that presses the pad against the fin loses power or malfunctions, the brake does not engage. The train passes through without slowing down. This is a problem that parks solved with redundant systems and fail-safe designs, but the fundamental failure mode existed.

Friction brakes also could not be finely tuned. You got a certain amount of braking force based on the pad pressure, and that was it. Adjusting for different train weights, speeds, or weather conditions required manual intervention.

## Enter Eddy Currents

Magnetic brakes exploit a physics phenomenon called eddy currents. Here is how it works, without equations:

When a conductive metal (like aluminum or copper) moves through a magnetic field, the changing magnetic field induces tiny electrical currents in the metal. These are eddy currents. Those currents create their own magnetic field, and that secondary field opposes the original magnetic field. The result: a force that resists the motion of the metal through the magnetic field.

In plain language: move a piece of metal past a magnet, and the magnet pushes back against the metal's motion. The faster the metal moves, the stronger the push-back. The slower the metal moves, the weaker the push-back. The braking force is proportional to speed.

This means magnetic brakes are strongest when the train is going fastest (when you need them most) and weakest when the train is going slowest (when you need them least). That is a natural, elegant braking curve that friction brakes cannot replicate.

## How It Works on a Coaster

On a modern roller coaster, magnetic brakes consist of two components:

**The magnets.** Banks of powerful permanent magnets (usually rare-earth neodymium magnets) are mounted on the track in the brake sections. They are typically arranged in pairs, one on each side of the track, creating a magnetic field that spans the gap between them.

**The brake fin.** A metal fin (usually aluminum or copper) is attached to the underside of each coaster train. This fin passes between the magnet banks as the train moves through the brake section.

When the train enters the brake section, the aluminum fin passes between the magnet banks. The changing magnetic field induces eddy currents in the fin, which create a resistive force that slows the train. No contact. No friction. No wear. The fin and the magnets never touch each other.

The braking force depends on three things: the strength of the magnets, the speed of the train, and the distance between the magnets and the fin. Parks can adjust braking force by changing the magnet spacing or by using retractable magnet assemblies that move the magnets closer to or further from the fin.

## Why They Cannot Fail Open

This is the property that made magnetic brakes revolutionary for safety.

Permanent magnets are always magnetic. They do not need power. They do not need actuators. They do not need compressed air. They are magnets, and they are always on. If you mount permanent magnets on the track, any conductive fin that passes through them will be slowed. Period. There is no scenario where power loss or mechanical failure causes the magnets to stop being magnetic.

Compare this to friction brakes, which need an active mechanical system to press the pad against the fin. If that system loses power, the pad does not press, and the brake is open. With magnetic brakes, the default state is braking. The only way to let a train pass through at full speed is to physically retract the magnets away from the fin, which requires active energy input. If anything fails, the magnets stay in the braking position, and the train is slowed.

This is called a "fail-safe" design. The system defaults to the safest state when anything goes wrong. Friction brakes had to be engineered to fail safe (springs that close the brakes on power loss). Magnetic brakes are inherently fail-safe by physics.

## The Limitations

Magnetic brakes are not perfect. They have real limitations that coaster designers work around:

**They cannot hold a train stationary.** Because the braking force is proportional to speed, a stationary train has zero speed and therefore zero magnetic braking force. Magnetic brakes can slow a train to a crawl, but they cannot stop it completely and hold it in place. This is why coaster brake sections use magnetic brakes for speed reduction and then a small set of friction or mechanical brakes (tire drives, pin brakes) to bring the train to a complete stop and hold it.

**They generate heat.** The eddy currents convert kinetic energy into heat in the brake fin. On a single pass, this is negligible. On a high-throughput ride running all day, the fins can get warm. This is managed through fin material selection (aluminum dissipates heat well) and by distributing braking across multiple brake sections.

**They add weight.** Neodymium magnets are dense. A full brake section with multiple magnet banks adds significant weight to the track structure, which requires beefier supports. This is a minor engineering consideration, not a deal-breaker, but it does affect track design.

**They degrade slightly over time.** Permanent magnets lose a fraction of their strength over decades (roughly 1% per decade for quality neodymium magnets at operating temperatures). This is so slow that it is essentially negligible for coaster applications, but it does mean the magnets have a lifespan measured in decades, not infinity.

## Linear Synchronous Motors: Magnetic Brakes in Reverse

The same physics that makes magnetic brakes work also makes magnetic launch systems work. Linear Synchronous Motors (LSMs) and Linear Induction Motors (LIMs) use electromagnets instead of permanent magnets, and instead of resisting the train's motion, they push it forward.

An LSM launch section looks similar to a magnetic brake section: magnet banks on the track, metal fins on the train. But instead of permanent magnets creating a static field, LSMs use electromagnets whose polarity switches rapidly, creating a traveling magnetic wave that pushes the train fin forward. By controlling the timing and polarity of the electromagnets, the launch system can precisely control the acceleration profile.

Many modern launched coasters use LSMs for the launch and permanent magnet brakes for the stop. The same physics, applied in opposite directions.

## What Changed

Before magnetic brakes, high-speed coasters were engineering challenges from a safety perspective. The faster a ride went, the more robust (and maintenance-intensive) the braking systems needed to be. Friction brake pads on a 70+ mph coaster wore out fast and needed frequent replacement.

Magnetic brakes removed that constraint. Rides could go faster because the braking system was inherently reliable, low-maintenance, and fail-safe. This directly enabled the era of 90+ mph coasters, launched coasters, and multi-launch coasters that dominate the modern industry.

It also reduced operating costs. A set of neodymium magnets on a brake run costs more upfront than friction brake pads, but the magnets last decades. The annual maintenance cost on magnetic brakes is close to zero compared to friction systems that need pad replacement, hydraulic service, and actuator maintenance multiple times per season.

## Spotting Them at the Park

Next time you ride a coaster, look at the track in the brake sections (the final slowdown before the station, and any mid-course brake runs). You will see shiny metallic blocks mounted in pairs on either side of the track. Those are the magnet banks. And the flat metal strip running along the underside of the train between the wheel assemblies is the brake fin.

On older coasters, you might see pneumatic actuators next to the brake sections (cylinders that push brake pads). On modern coasters, you will see the clean, compact magnet banks with no visible moving parts. The difference is immediately obvious once you know what to look for.

The silent, smooth deceleration you feel at the end of every modern coaster ride is eddy current physics doing its job. No friction, no wear, no failure modes. Just magnets.
