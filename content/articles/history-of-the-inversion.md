The first modern looping coaster opened in 1976. Within fifty years, the industry went from "can we get riders upside down without hurting them" to designing inversions so smooth you barely notice you are inverted. The evolution of the inversion is one of the most interesting engineering stories in the amusement industry, because every generation solved problems that the previous generation did not know existed.

## The False Start: Circular Loops

The idea of a looping roller coaster is older than you might think. In the late 1800s and early 1900s, a handful of rides attempted circular loops. The problem was physics. A perfect circle requires higher G-forces at the bottom of the loop than a human body can safely handle. To maintain enough speed to make it over the top of a circular loop, the train enters the bottom at a speed that generates 6 to 12 positive Gs. Fighter pilots black out at sustained 9 Gs. Riders in the early circular loop coasters were getting compressed into their seats hard enough to cause whiplash, nausea, and potential spinal injuries.

These early loops were quickly abandoned. The concept was proven (you could flip riders upside down on a track), but the execution was dangerous.

## The Clothoid: The Shape That Changed Everything

The breakthrough came from a mathematical curve called a clothoid (also called a Cornu spiral or Euler spiral). A clothoid loop is not circular. It is taller and narrower, like a teardrop or an inverted water drop. The radius of the curve decreases as you go up and increases as you come down.

Why does this matter? Because the tighter radius at the top means the train can maintain positive G-forces at the top of the loop with less entry speed. And the wider radius at the bottom means the G-forces at the bottom are lower and more comfortable. A clothoid loop can put riders through a full 360-degree inversion at a peak of about 3.5 to 4.5 Gs at the bottom, which is intense but safe. A circular loop of the same height would require 6+ Gs.

The clothoid loop made modern inversions possible. Every vertical loop on every coaster you have ever ridden uses some variation of this shape.

## 1976: Arrow's Corkscrew

Arrow Development (later Arrow Dynamics) opened the first modern inverting coaster in 1976 at Knott's Berry Farm: the Corkscrew. It featured two corkscrew inversions (360-degree barrel rolls along the direction of travel) over the park midway.

The Corkscrew was a sensation. People lined up for hours. The concept of going upside down on a roller coaster was terrifying and thrilling in equal measure. Arrow followed up by selling corkscrew models to parks worldwide.

But Arrow's early inversions had a problem that enthusiasts know well: headbanging. The track transitions into and out of the inversions were not perfectly smooth. Combined with the rigid over-the-shoulder restraints on Arrow's trains, every transition became a potential collision between the rider's head and the hard plastic ear guards on the restraint. The rides worked. They just were not comfortable.

## 1976-1988: The Loop Arms Race

After the Corkscrew, the industry went into a loop frenzy. Arrow built larger looping coasters: the Loop (Great American Revolution at Six Flags Magic Mountain, 1976) had the first modern clothoid vertical loop. Schwarzkopf in Germany was building its own looping coasters with smoother track profiles and lower G-forces. Vekoma entered the market with its boomerangs and SLCs.

By the mid-1980s, the inversion count was the marketing metric. Parks wanted more loops, more corkscrews, more ways to flip riders upside down. Arrow's Viper at Six Flags Magic Mountain (1990) had seven inversions. The sheer number of inversions became a selling point, even though more inversions did not necessarily mean a better ride.

The late 1980s also saw the first attempts at different inversion types beyond the basic vertical loop and corkscrew:

**The cobra roll (or batwing).** Two half-inversions back to back, creating a shape that resembles a cobra's hood. The rider goes inverted, comes back to upright, and then goes inverted again in the opposite direction. Arrow and Vekoma both used this element.

**The bowtie.** Similar to a cobra roll but with a different entry and exit angle. Less common, but it showed that designers were thinking about inversions as creative design elements, not just "go upside down."

## 1992: B&M Arrives and Changes the Rules

Bolliger and Mabillard's first coaster, Batman: The Ride at Six Flags Great Adventure (1992), was an inverted coaster with five inversions. It was smooth. Shockingly smooth. The inversions flowed into each other with none of the jarring transitions that characterized Arrow's designs. The vest-style restraints (later replaced with over-the-shoulder on the Batman clones, but the early B&M inverts used a more open design) allowed riders to experience the inversions without headbanging.

B&M achieved this by using computer-optimized track profiles and their proprietary track manufacturing process. B&M track is box-spine (a rectangular cross-section) rather than Arrow's tubular spine, and the welds and connections are precision-machined. The result is a track surface that is smoother at a micro level, which translates to smoother transitions at a macro level.

With Batman and subsequent inverted coasters (Raptor, Montu, Alpengeist), B&M proved that inversions could be intense without being painful. This was transformative. Parks no longer had to worry about rider complaints about headbanging when they ordered a looping coaster. B&M's inversions worked, they were comfortable, and they were re-rideable.

## The Inversion Catalog

Over the following decades, coaster designers invented dozens of inversion types. Here are the major ones:

**Vertical Loop.** The original. A clothoid-shaped full 360-degree loop. Still the most common inversion on coasters worldwide.

**Corkscrew / Barrel Roll.** A 360-degree roll along the axis of travel. The rider rotates while continuing forward. Smooth when well-designed, jerky on older installations.

**Zero-G Roll.** Similar to a barrel roll, but the track is shaped so that riders experience weightlessness (zero G-forces) at the top of the roll. The sensation is floating while spinning. B&M popularized this element.

**Cobra Roll (Batwing).** Two half-inversions that send riders inverted and back. The visual shape resembles a cobra's hood. Common on B&M inverts and Vekoma SLCs.

**Immelmann.** Named after the World War I aerial maneuver. The rider enters a half-loop upward and then rolls out at the top, exiting in the opposite direction. B&M uses this frequently on dive coasters and hyper coasters with inversions.

**Dive Loop.** The reverse of an Immelmann. The rider rolls inverted and then pulls through a half-loop downward. Common on B&M sit-down and floorless coasters.

**Pretzel Loop.** Unique to B&M flying coasters (where riders are in a face-down position). The train dives down into a loop, passes through the bottom face-down, and pulls up the other side. The sensation is extreme because the G-forces push you into the harness in the face-down position. The pretzel loop on Tatsu at Six Flags Magic Mountain pulls approximately 4.5 Gs and is one of the most intense moments on any coaster.

**Zero-G Stall.** An extended inverted section where the track rolls 180 degrees, holds the rider upside down for a sustained period, and then rolls back. Popularized by RMC (Iron Gwazi, Steel Vengeance) and Intamin (Velocicoaster). The zero-G stall has become one of the most celebrated modern inversion types because the sustained hangtime is exhilarating.

**Heartline Roll.** The track rotates around the rider's center of gravity (their heart line) rather than around the track center. This means the rider does not move up or down during the roll. They simply rotate in place. The sensation is pure rotation without the positive G-forces of a traditional roll. Intamin and B&M both use heartline rolls.

**Cutback.** A half-inversion that reverses the rider's direction. Used on B&M inverted and wing coasters.

**Norwegian Loop.** Essentially a dive loop followed immediately by an Immelmann. Named because it was first used on a coaster in Norway. Creates a dramatic double-inversion sequence.

## The Modern Philosophy: Quality Over Quantity

The inversion arms race of the 1990s and 2000s (where parks marketed coasters by their inversion count) has largely ended. Modern coaster design treats inversions as tools in a larger toolkit, not as the primary selling point.

RMC's approach is representative: their coasters might have 3 to 4 inversions, but each one is a different type (a zero-G stall, a barrel roll, a zero-G roll) and serves a different purpose in the ride's pacing. The inversions are integrated into the layout rather than strung together for the sake of counting.

B&M's newer installations also reflect this shift. Their dive coasters have 2 to 3 inversions, not 7. Their hyper coasters sometimes include a single zero-G roll as a surprise element in an airtime-focused layout. The inversion is a punctuation mark, not the sentence.

The result is that modern inversions feel better than the marathon loop-fests of the 1990s. A single well-designed zero-G stall on an RMC delivers more memorable sensation than seven corkscrews on a Vekoma SLC. The industry learned that the quality of the inversion matters more than the quantity.

## What Riders Feel

For non-enthusiasts, all inversions feel like "going upside down." For riders who have experienced enough variety, inversions are a spectrum:

**High positive G inversions** (vertical loops, pretzel loops) push you into the seat. You feel heavy. Your vision can dim if the Gs are strong enough. The sensation is compression.

**Zero-G inversions** (zero-G rolls, zero-G stalls) feel like floating while spinning. Your body lifts out of the seat. It is the closest sensation to actual weightlessness that a coaster can produce.

**Snap inversions** (quick barrel rolls, corkscrews at speed) are over before you fully process them. The sensation is disorientation. You went upside down and back in under a second.

**Hang inversions** (stalls, sustained inverted sections) are the most psychologically intense. Your brain knows you are upside down. Gravity is pulling you into the restraint. The longer it lasts, the more your brain insists that something is wrong. This is why zero-G stalls have become so popular. They exploit the most primal reaction to being inverted.

## The Next Chapter

Inversion design is still evolving. RMC's barrel roll drops (where the first drop itself includes a roll) are a recent innovation that would have been engineering fiction twenty years ago. Intamin's mosasaurus roll on Velocicoaster combined an inversion with a water flyover and a thematic moment. Vekoma's new-generation rides include inversions that rival B&M for smoothness.

The question is no longer "can we flip riders upside down?" It is "what can we make them feel while they are upside down?" That shift, from engineering challenge to artistic expression, is the real story of the inversion.
