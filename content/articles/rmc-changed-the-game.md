There's a moment in every thoosie's life when they first ride an RMC. For a lot of us, it's the moment this hobby goes from "I like roller coasters" to "I need to ride every roller coaster." Nobody else in the industry is doing what Rocky Mountain Construction does. And the wildest part is how it all started.

## A Shop in Idaho

RMC isn't based in Orlando or some European engineering hub. They're out of Hayden, Idaho. Population: roughly 15,000. Fred Grubb founded the company doing what you'd expect from a small operation in the Pacific Northwest: toboggans, mountain coasters, maintenance work. Bread and butter stuff.

The turning point came when coaster designer Alan Schilke got involved. Schilke had a vision for a new type of track, one that could replace the wooden rails on aging woodies with a steel alternative. Not just re-tracking them for smoothness, but completely re-profiling the layout. New drops. New inversions. New everything. All while keeping the original wooden structure standing.

The technology was called Iron Horse track, later refined into I-Box track. And it changed everything.

## New Texas Giant: The Proof of Concept

In 2011, Six Flags Over Texas reopened their Texas Giant after a full RMC conversion. The original had been a 14-story wooden coaster that opened in 1990, once ranked the best wooden coaster in the world. By the 2000s, it had gotten rough. Really rough. The kind of rough where you leave the ride checking for bruises.

RMC kept the wooden support structure, tore off the old track, re-profiled the layout with steeper drops and overbanked turns up to 115 degrees, and laid down their new steel I-Box track. The result was New Texas Giant, and it was a revelation.

Smooth where the original was punishing. Aggressive where the original was tame. It won Best New Ride at the 2011 Golden Ticket Awards, and suddenly every park with a deteriorating woodie was making phone calls to Idaho.

## The Conversion Era

After New Texas Giant proved the concept, the conversions came fast.

Iron Rattler opened at Six Flags Fiesta Texas in 2013, transforming the aging Rattler into something with a 171-foot drop and a quarry wall fly-by that still makes people lose their minds. Twisted Colossus hit Six Flags Magic Mountain in 2015, turning the classic dual-track Colossus into a racing/dueling hybrid with a Mobius loop layout. One station, two tracks, and when the timing lines up and both trains are neck and neck through the high five element? There's nothing else like it in the world.

Wicked Cyclone at Six Flags New England. Storm Chaser at Kentucky Kingdom. Joker at Six Flags Discovery Kingdom. Each one took a wooden coaster that had seen better days and turned it into something people would plan entire trips around.

Then came Lightning Rod at Dollywood in 2016. This one was different. Not a conversion of an existing woodie, but a ground-up build using RMC's Topper Track on a wooden structure. And it launched. The first launched wooden coaster ever built. The combination of RMC's wild layout design with a launch instead of a chain lift was absolutely unhinged. (The launch system was famously temperamental and eventually got swapped for a high-speed chain lift in 2024, but the ride itself is still incredible.)

## Steel Vengeance: The Masterpiece

If you ask 100 thoosies to name the best roller coaster in the world, a huge percentage of them are going to say Steel Vengeance. There's a reason for that.

Cedar Point's Mean Streak was a notorious rough woodie, the kind of ride that enthusiasts actively avoided. RMC got their hands on it and what came out in 2018 was something that set 10 world records on opening day. 205 feet tall. 74 mph. Four inversions. 5,740 feet of track. And the stat that matters most: nearly 30 seconds of airtime. More than any other roller coaster on the planet.

Steel Vengeance doesn't just have airtime. It is airtime. The back row on the first drop ejects you out of your seat so hard that your brain takes a second to catch up with your body. The wave turn throws you sideways. The inversions snap you through with a violence that should feel aggressive but somehow feels graceful. And just when you think it's winding down, there's another hill, another moment of weightlessness, another reason to immediately get back in line.

It's two and a half minutes long and every single second of it is doing something. There's no filler. No boring straight sections. No "well, the second half is kind of slow." It's relentless from the drop to the brake run.

## Iron Gwazi and the Modern Era

If Steel Vengeance made thoosies fall in love with RMC, Iron Gwazi at Busch Gardens Tampa proved it wasn't a one-time thing. Opening in 2022 after COVID delays, it features a 206-foot drop at 91 degrees (beyond vertical, which means you're literally past straight down) and speeds up to 76 mph. It won Best New Roller Coaster at the Golden Ticket Awards in 2022.

Iron Gwazi feels different from Steel Vengeance. Where SteVe is a marathon of sustained airtime, Iron Gwazi is more about intensity in bursts. The first drop is genuinely violent in the best way possible. The outer banked turn feels like the rails are about to disappear. It's a shorter ride but every element hits harder per second.

And that's the thing about RMC. They don't have a formula. Yes, there are signature moves (the wave turn, the stall, the double-up). But each ride has its own personality. Twisted Colossus is playful and theatrical. Steel Vengeance is an endurance test of joy. Iron Gwazi is controlled chaos. They're all unmistakably RMC, but none of them feel like copies.

## Why RMC Matters

Before RMC, the conversation around aging wooden coasters was usually: "tear it down or let it rot." Parks with rough woodies had a liability and a maintenance headache. Enthusiasts avoided them. GP complained about them. There wasn't a great answer.

RMC created the answer. Take the existing structure, keep the footprint, keep the height, and build something on top of it that's better than what could be designed from scratch. It's recycling, engineering, and artistry all at once.

And beyond conversions, their ground-up builds (Goliath at Six Flags Great America, Wildfire at Kolmarden in Sweden) proved they could design from a blank canvas just as well. RMC didn't just find a niche. They created a new category.

## What's Next

As of early 2026, RMC has built or refurbished 29 roller coasters worldwide. Every single one is currently operating. That's a track record (pun intended) that no other manufacturer can match for sheer consistency of quality.

The enthusiast community's relationship with RMC is something you don't really see with other manufacturers. B&M makes incredible coasters. Intamin pushes boundaries. Vekoma has had a serious glow-up in recent years. But nobody generates the same level of hype as RMC. An RMC announcement at any park is instant front-page news in the coaster world.

And honestly, they've earned it. When you consistently deliver rides that end up in people's top 5 worldwide, year after year, park after park, that kind of reputation isn't hype. It's just reality.

If you haven't ridden an RMC yet, put it at the top of your list. Doesn't matter which one. You'll understand as soon as the train crests the lift hill and the track disappears beneath you.

Welcome to the church of RMC. We don't do smooth wooden coasters here. We do better.
