Ask a hundred thoosies what makes a great roller coaster and you'll get a hundred answers. But if you listen long enough, you'll notice that most of those answers fall into two camps.

Camp one: airtime. The feeling of weightlessness. Floating out of your seat. Negative g's that lift your stomach into your throat and make you feel, for just a second, like gravity forgot about you.

Camp two: intensity. Positive g's. Whip. Force that pins you into your seat and makes your vision tunnel and pushes the air out of your lungs. The raw, physical, almost violent sensation of being flung through a turn at speeds your body was not designed for.

These two things are not the same. They produce completely different sensations. And the coasters that prioritize one tend to be very different machines from the ones that prioritize the other.

So which one actually matters more?

## The Airtime Argument

Airtime enthusiasts will tell you that nothing else in the coaster world compares to a well-executed ejector moment. And honestly, it's hard to argue with them.

Ejector airtime is the kind where you're not just floating, you're being thrown out of your seat. The lap bar catches you. Your legs lift off the floor. Your hands go up not because you're posing for the on-ride photo but because holding on feels wrong when the rest of your body is trying to leave the train.

**El Toro at Six Flags Great Adventure** is the ride that converted an entire generation of enthusiasts into airtime chasers. The Rolling Thunder hill is legendary, a moment so violent in its ejector that people have described it as "being picked up by the track and thrown at the sky." The Intamin prefab wooden track delivers hits that are sharp, sudden, and relentless.

**Steel Vengeance** took that concept and stretched it to nearly 30 seconds of total airtime across a two-and-a-half-minute ride. That's not a coaster with good airtime. That's an airtime delivery system that happens to also be a coaster.

**Skyrush at Hersheypark** is the ride that airtime purists point to when they want to separate the casual fans from the committed. The wing seats deliver airtime so aggressive that the community literally nicknamed it "Thighcrush" because of how hard the lap bar digs in. It's not comfortable. It's not gentle. But the airtime? Unmatched. People rank it in their top 5 worldwide despite the restraints, not because of them.

The airtime crowd's argument is simple: the thing that makes coasters unique as a thrill experience is the manipulation of gravity. No other ride type, no other recreational activity, can give you that sensation of weightlessness at speed. Airtime is the thing coasters do that nothing else can.

## The Intensity Argument

On the other side, you've got the people who want to feel every g-force the ride can generate. These are the riders who rank Intimidator 305 above everything else and genuinely mean it.

**Intimidator 305 at Kings Dominion** is the poster child for intensity. That first turn after the 305-foot drop pulls so many g's that riders regularly gray out. Your peripheral vision collapses. The world narrows to a pinhole. And then the ride keeps going, snapping through quick direction changes and low-to-the-ground transitions at speeds that make the landscape blur. It's not trying to float you. It's trying to crush you. And some people absolutely love that.

**Fury 325 at Carowinds** is intensity delivered differently. Instead of sharp, punishing g-forces, Fury gives you sustained speed. The turns are wide and sweeping, but you're taking them at 95 mph. The g's aren't violent, they're persistent. You feel pressed into your seat through the entire ride, and the sheer scale of the thing, how far off the ground you are, how fast the world is moving past you, creates a sensation that pure airtime rides don't replicate.

**Maverick at Cedar Point** splits the difference in an interesting way. It's got airtime. It's got intensity. It's got a heartline roll and a tunnel launch and transitions so fast that your neck muscles are the limiting factor. Maverick is what happens when a designer says "why not both?" and then cranks everything to maximum.

The intensity argument goes like this: anybody can do a bunny hop. Not every coaster can make your body feel like it weighs 400 pounds. The physical, visceral, almost combative experience of a high-g ride is what separates a great coaster from a good one.

## The Real Answer (Sort Of)

Here's the thing. The best coasters in the world don't pick one. They balance both.

Look at the rides that consistently top enthusiast polls worldwide. Steel Vengeance has relentless airtime but also has inversions, lateral forces, and moments of genuine intensity. Iron Gwazi alternates between violent ejector and high-speed sustained forces. Velocicoaster at Universal delivers airtime on the top hat, intensity on the inversions, and both simultaneously through the mosasaurus roll.

The coasters that only do one thing well tend to be divisive. Skyrush is a top-five ride for some people and an actively unpleasant experience for others. Intimidator 305 inspires devotion from its fans and indifference from people who just want to float. Single-note rides attract single-note audiences.

The rides that blend forces, that give you a massive airtime hill followed by a whip-snap helix followed by a sustained high-speed sweeping turn followed by another ejector moment, those are the ones that everyone agrees on. Because they're giving you everything. Not just one flavor of thrill, but the full spectrum.

## What Your Preference Says About You

This is where it gets fun. Your preference between airtime and intensity actually says something about what you value in a coaster experience.

Airtime lovers tend to value freedom. The hands-up, wind-in-your-face, I-could-fly feeling. They gravitate toward open restraints, stadium seating, and rides that feel liberating. They want the coaster to release them from gravity, not press them deeper into it.

Intensity lovers tend to value control. The feeling of the ride asserting its dominance over your body. They appreciate how a coaster can make you feel small, how 4+ g's remind you that you're a tiny human strapped to a machine moving faster than your brain can process. There's a strange comfort in surrendering to forces that powerful.

Neither is wrong. They're just different relationships with the same physics.

## The Rides That Changed Minds

If you think you're firmly in one camp, there's probably a ride out there that could flip your opinion.

If you only chase airtime: ride Intimidator 305. Ride it in the front row. Feel that first turn try to take your consciousness. Then tell me intensity doesn't matter.

If you only chase intensity: ride El Toro. Sit in the back. Hit the Rolling Thunder hill and feel your entire body separate from the seat. Then tell me airtime doesn't matter.

If you've ridden both and still have a strong preference, that's fine too. That's what makes this hobby endlessly debatable. Two people can ride the exact same coaster back to back and walk away with completely different rankings because they're literally feeling the ride differently based on what they value.

## Where It Lands

At the end of the day, the argument between airtime and intensity is the coaster equivalent of asking "Beatles or Stones?" The correct answer is "both, and also a bunch of other stuff." But the debate itself is half the fun.

Your top 10 list reveals what you prioritize. Is it stacked with RMCs and Intamin airtime machines? Or is it B&M gigas, Schwarzkopf loops, and that one coaster at that one park that grayed you out so hard you saw stars?

There's no wrong answer. There's only your answer. And defending it to your friends in the queue for the next hour is one of the best parts of being an enthusiast.

So. Airtime or intensity?

Choose your side. Just know that someone in the back row of Steel Vengeance right now is experiencing both and not bothering to pick.
