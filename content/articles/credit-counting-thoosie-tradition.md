You rode Millennium Force. You survived Kingda Ka. You laughed your way through Twisted Colossus. And somewhere along the way, you started counting.

Not laps. Not parks. Credits.

If you're reading this, there's a decent chance you already know your number. Maybe it's 47. Maybe it's 312. Maybe you hit 100 last summer and texted your group chat about it like you just summited Everest. And honestly? You kind of did.

Credit counting is one of those things that sounds weird to people outside the hobby and makes total sense to everyone inside it. So let's talk about what it actually is, where it came from, and why thoosies treat their credit count like a badge of honor.

## So What Counts as a Credit?

A credit is one unique roller coaster ridden. Ride Steel Vengeance once, that's one credit. Ride it 47 times in a day (respect), that's still one credit. The count tracks how many different coasters you've been on, not how many times you've ridden them.

Sounds simple, right? It is. Until it isn't.

The debates start when you get into the gray areas. Does a kiddie coaster count? Most people say yes, a coaster is a coaster. What about a powered coaster that technically uses motors the whole way? That one splits the community pretty hard. What about alpine coasters? Go-karts with a chain lift? Rides that got re-tracked so heavily they're basically a new coaster?

Every enthusiast draws their line somewhere. Some people count everything with a RCDB page. Others only count stuff they'd actually call a roller coaster if someone asked. There's no governing body handing out official rulings here. Your count, your rules.

The one thing basically everyone agrees on: you have to actually ride it. Standing in the station doesn't count. Watching the POV on YouTube definitely doesn't count. You sat in the seat, the restraint came down, you went over the hill. Credit earned.

## Where Did This Start?

Credit counting has been around as long as there have been people weird enough to drive six hours to ride a relocated Vekoma Boomerang. Which is to say, a long time.

The tradition really picked up steam in the early internet era. Forums like CoasterBuzz, Theme Park Review, and the old rec.roller-coaster newsgroups gave enthusiasts a place to compare numbers for the first time. Suddenly your credit count wasn't just something you tracked in a notebook. It was your forum signature. Your identity.

Sites like Coaster-Count and Captain Coaster formalized it even more, giving people a database to log every ride against the full global coaster census. You could see exactly which credits you were missing at a park, plan trips around filling gaps, and compare your count against friends or the wider community.

But the culture around counting predates all of that. ACE (American Coaster Enthusiasts) members have been keeping lists in spiral notebooks since the 1970s. The tools got better, but the impulse was always there. When you love something this much, you keep track.

## Why People Actually Do It

Here's the thing that non-enthusiasts don't get: credit counting isn't really about the number. The number is just the scoreboard. What it actually tracks is your history with this hobby.

Every credit is a memory. Credit #1 was probably something at your home park when you were seven. Credit #50 might have been the first time you traveled specifically for a coaster. Credit #100 was probably planned, probably special, probably a ride you'd been dreaming about for years.

Your credit list is basically a diary. Scroll through it and you'll remember the road trips, the friends who came with you, the rain delays, the walk-on marathons, the time you drove three hours for a coaster that turned out to be mid but you still had an incredible day because the park was empty and the food was good and the sunset from the top of the Ferris wheel was perfect.

That's what credit counting is really about. The number just gives you a framework to collect those experiences.

## The Social Side

Credit counts are social currency in the enthusiast community, and people are generally cool about it. Someone with 800 credits isn't going to gatekeep someone with 30. If anything, high-count riders tend to get genuinely excited when someone's just starting out, because they remember what it felt like to discover this hobby.

That said, there's a healthy competitive element too. Planning a trip around "I need six more credits to hit 200" is a real thing. Racing a friend to a milestone number is a real thing. Driving to a random fair to ride a Wacky Worm because it technically counts is very much a real thing.

And then there's the flex. Posting a screenshot of your count hitting a round number, sharing your top 10, ranking your credits from best to worst. That's half the fun of the hobby right there.

## The Debates That Never Die

Every enthusiast community has its recurring arguments, and credit counting has some classics:

**"Do kiddie coasters count?"** Yes. Next question. (OK fine, some people disagree, but a credit is a credit.)

**"Does a re-tracked coaster count as a new credit?"** This one is actually interesting. When RMC converts a wooden coaster into a hybrid (like Mean Streak becoming Steel Vengeance), most people count that as a new credit because the layout, track, and experience are completely different. But where's the line? If a park re-profiles three turns and adds a zero-g roll, is that a new coaster?

**"Quality or quantity?"** Some people chase numbers. They'll fly across the country to knock out 15 credits at a park they'll never visit again. Others only care about riding the best stuff and couldn't care less about their total count. Neither approach is wrong. Both are fun.

**"Does it matter if the coaster is defunct now?"** Your credit stands forever. If you rode the OG Dueling Dragons before it became Hagrid's, that credit is yours. Nobody can take it from you. Defunct credits are actually some of the coolest ones to have because they're irreplaceable.

## Why It Matters for TrackR

We built TrackR because we're credit counters too. And honestly, the existing tools for tracking credits haven't kept up with the hobby. Spreadsheets work but they're ugly. The existing websites are functional but feel like they were built in 2012 (because some of them were).

Your credit count deserves better than a checkbox on a database. Every credit has a story, a rating, a memory attached to it. The way you track your coaster life should feel as good as riding them.

That's what we're building.
