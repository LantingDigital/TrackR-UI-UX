Tell me your home park and I'll tell you what kind of enthusiast you are.

That's not a party trick. It's basically true. The park you grew up visiting, the one you know by heart, the one where you can navigate the midway with your eyes closed and you know exactly which bathroom is the cleanest and which food stand has the best fries, that park shaped your entire understanding of what roller coasters are supposed to feel like.

And you probably don't even realize how much.

## The First Standard

Your home park sets your baseline. It's not just the first park you went to. It's the park that taught you what a roller coaster IS. The forces, the speed, the height, the pacing, whatever your home park's star coaster delivered, that became your definition of "good."

If you grew up on **Cedar Point**, your baseline is absurdly high. You think every park should have 15+ coasters. You think a 200-foot coaster is "mid-sized." You've been on Millennium Force so many times that a 300-foot drop at 93 mph feels normal. When you visit other parks and their star attraction is a 120-foot B&M invert, you're not trying to be a snob, but your internal scale is just calibrated differently.

If you grew up on **Kings Island**, you value variety and soul. Your park has a legendary woodie (The Beast), a world-class giga (Orion), elite B&M inverts (Banshee), and one of the best GCI woodies ever built (Mystic Timbers). You also have the Son of Beast scar tissue, which means you know that ambition without execution can go very wrong. Kings Island kids tend to appreciate balance. They want a park that does everything well, not just one thing spectacularly.

If **Six Flags Magic Mountain** was your proving ground, speed and intensity are your love language. You grew up on X2 (the most unhinged ride experience in North America), Twisted Colossus (the best racing coaster ever built, fight me), Tatsu (the pretzel loop that redefines "intense"), and a collection of loopers that taught you inversions at a very young age. Magic Mountain kids are the ones who shrug when someone describes a coaster as "intense." Your threshold is just different.

If you came up at **Busch Gardens Tampa**, theming matters to you more than you realize. You spent your childhood walking through one of the most beautiful parks in the country, riding coasters that were integrated into lush landscaping and themed areas. SheiKra, Cheetah Hunt, Montu, Iron Gwazi, they're not just rides, they're embedded in environments that make the whole experience feel premium. When you visit a Six Flags with a coaster standing in a parking lot, something feels off. You might not be able to articulate why. It's because your home park taught you that context matters.

## The Bias You Don't Know You Have

Here's the uncomfortable truth: your home park creates a bias that's almost impossible to fully correct for.

If your home park had great B&M coasters, you probably overrate B&M as a manufacturer. You associate B&M with "good" because B&M IS your definition of good. When someone criticizes B&M for being "too safe" or "lacking ejector airtime," you're confused, because the B&M rides you grew up on were the most exciting things in your world.

If your home park was an RMC park (or got an RMC conversion), you might have unrealistic expectations for what every coaster should deliver. Once you've ridden Steel Vengeance or Iron Gwazi, going back to a standard wooden coaster feels like watching TV on a flip phone. That's not the woodie's fault. Your calibration is just off because RMC ruined you.

If your home park was smaller, maybe a **Kennywood** or a **Holiday World** or a **Knoebels**, you developed an appreciation for charm, history, and value that big-park kids sometimes miss. You know that a park doesn't need 17 coasters to be worth visiting. You know that a well-maintained classic can deliver more joy than a neglected headliner. And you probably have very strong opinions about how commercial the bigger parks feel, because your home park proved you don't need a $200 million budget to create something people love.

## The Home Park Defense Instinct

Nothing reveals a thoosie's home park faster than a perceived slight against it. Say something critical about someone's home park and watch what happens. The response is immediate, passionate, and usually involves a detailed defense of every ride in the lineup plus an explanation of why the park is actually underrated and people just don't give it enough credit.

"Actually, Dorney Park has really improved in the last few years and Iron Menace is legitimate and also Talon is one of the best inverts in the country and the waterpark is incredible."

"Actually, Six Flags Great Adventure's coaster lineup is one of the deepest in the world, people just hate on it because of operations and the food, but the rides themselves are elite."

"Actually, Kings Dominion has been slept on for years and Intimidator 305 alone makes it worth the trip and also the park is beautiful in the fall."

Every one of those statements is said with the energy of a defense attorney in a murder trial. Because your home park is personal. It's your park. It's where you became an enthusiast. Criticizing it feels like criticizing a part of your identity, because it is.

## How It Shows Up in Your Rankings

Pull up any thoosie's top 20 coaster list and you'll see their home park fingerprints all over it.

Home park rides tend to be ranked higher than they "should" be, because familiarity breeds affection. You've ridden Diamondback 200 times. You know every hill, every transition, every moment where the wind hits differently depending on the row. That intimacy creates a bond that a one-and-done credit at another park can't match. The ride might be objectively comparable to five other B&M hypers, but it's YOUR B&M hyper, and that matters.

The counter-effect is also real. Some people underrate their home park coasters because they've ridden them so many times that the excitement has worn off. "Millennium Force? Yeah, it's fine. I've ridden it 300 times." Cool, you've ridden the fourth-tallest coaster in America 300 times and you're calling it "fine." That's home park privilege talking.

## When You Visit Someone Else's Home Park

The most interesting coaster conversations happen when two thoosies from different home parks visit each other's parks.

A Cedar Point regular visits Kings Island and is blown away by The Beast at night because Cedar Point doesn't have anything like it. A Kings Island regular visits Cedar Point and can't believe the sheer volume of world-class coasters packed into one peninsula. Both of them walk away saying "OK, I get it now" about the other park, and both go home still preferring their own.

That's the beautiful thing about this hobby. Home park loyalty isn't about thinking your park is objectively the best. It's about the relationship you have with a place. The thousands of hours you've spent there. The memories attached to every ride, every food stand, every parking lot arrival where you see the skyline of coasters and feel something in your chest.

## It Goes Deeper Than Coasters

Your home park didn't just shape your coaster taste. It shaped your park expectations.

If your home park had good food, you're the person who judges every park by its food. If your home park had terrible operations (long dispatches, constant stacking, rides down for maintenance), you either developed patience or you developed strong opinions about ride ops. If your home park had great employees, friendly and enthusiastic and clearly happy to be there, you notice immediately when another park's staff feels disengaged.

Your tolerance for crowds, your feelings about flash passes and virtual queues, your expectations for parking and entry flow, even your preference for what time of day you arrive at a park, all of these were shaped by the rhythms of your home park.

## The Takeaway

Your home park is your coaster origin story. It's the lens you see every other park through, even when you try to be objective. It gave you your favorites, your biases, your standards, and your loyalty.

And there's nothing wrong with that. Having a home park you love, a place that feels like yours in a way that other parks never quite match, is one of the best parts of this hobby. It gives you a foundation. Somewhere you return to season after season, watching it change, growing with it, defending it against anyone who calls it overrated.

So the next time someone asks you what your home park is, pay attention to how you answer. The pride in your voice, the immediate urge to list what makes it great, the slight defensiveness when someone compares it unfavorably to another park.

That's not just fandom. That's identity. And every thoosie has it.

What's your home park?
