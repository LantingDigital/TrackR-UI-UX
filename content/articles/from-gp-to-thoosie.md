Nobody wakes up one morning and decides to be a roller coaster enthusiast. It's a pipeline. And by the time you realize you're in it, you're already too far gone.

If you're reading this, you're probably somewhere on the spectrum between "I really like roller coasters" and "I have opinions about B&M's recent output." Both are valid. But the journey from one to the other is weirdly universal, and almost everyone goes through the same stages.

Let's walk through it.

## Stage 1: The Casual

You go to theme parks. Maybe once a year, maybe twice. You ride the big rides, you eat a turkey leg, you buy a shirt. You have a great time. If someone asks your favorite roller coaster, you say whatever's at your home park. You don't know who manufactured it. You don't care. It goes fast and it's fun.

At this stage, you are what the enthusiast community affectionately (and sometimes not-so-affectionately) calls "GP," short for "General Public." You're the person who calls every coaster "a roller coaster" without distinguishing between a B&M hyper and a Vekoma boomerang. You think Space Mountain is intense. You've never heard of RCDB.

There is absolutely nothing wrong with being GP. Theme parks are designed for you. You're having the time the parks intended.

But something is about to change.

## Stage 2: The Awakening

Something clicks. Maybe you ride a coaster that's just on a completely different level from anything you've experienced before. Maybe it's your first RMC, or your first giga, or your first launch coaster. Whatever it is, you get off the ride and you don't just think "that was fun." You think "what was THAT."

You pull out your phone. You Google the ride. You find out it was made by Rocky Mountain Construction, or Bolliger & Mabillard, or Intamin, and you've never heard any of these names before but suddenly you want to know more. You click through to a coaster database. You see that the ride is 205 feet tall and hits 74 mph and has 27 seconds of airtime and you didn't know airtime was a measurable thing but now you need to know which coasters have the most of it.

This is the moment. Right here. The Google search after the ride. That's where it starts.

You might not recognize it at the time. You might think you're just looking up some facts about a ride you liked. But you're not. You're entering the pipeline. And the pipeline only moves in one direction.

## Stage 3: The Research Phase

This stage lasts anywhere from two weeks to six months, and it consumes you.

You start watching POV videos on YouTube. First it's just the coasters at parks you've been to. Then it's coasters at parks you want to visit. Then it's coasters at parks in other countries that you'll probably never visit but you need to know what they're like anyway.

You discover coaster YouTube. ElToroRyan, Airtime Thrills, Coaster Studios, Theme Park Crazy. You watch someone rank every coaster at Cedar Point and you've never been to Cedar Point but you have opinions about their ranking. Strong opinions.

You learn terminology. Airtime. Ejector. Floater. Laterals. Hangtime. Whip. Rattle. Trim brakes (the enemy). You start using these words in conversation and your friends look at you like you've joined a cult.

You find RCDB (Roller Coaster Database) and spend an evening clicking through every coaster at every park you've ever visited. You start mentally cataloguing which ones you've ridden. Wait, is that credit counting? You're credit counting now.

You join a Reddit community. Or a Discord. Or a forum. You lurk at first. You read trip reports from people who visited six parks in one week and you think "that sounds exhausting and amazing and I want to do it."

## Stage 4: The First Planned Trip

This is the stage that separates "enthusiast-curious" from "enthusiast." You plan a trip specifically for coasters.

Not "we're going to Orlando, might as well hit a park." Not "my family has season passes so we go on weekends." A trip where the primary purpose, the whole reason you're getting in the car or booking a flight, is to ride roller coasters.

Maybe it's a road trip to Cedar Point. Maybe it's a quick drive to a Six Flags you've never been to for the credits. Maybe it's a full multi-park trip hitting three states in four days. The specifics don't matter. What matters is that you looked at a map, identified parks, checked their coaster lineups, and decided this was worth your time and money.

When you make that first trip, you'll probably ride with an intensity you've never experienced before. You'll be at the gate at rope drop. You'll have a plan for which rides to hit first. You'll marathon your new favorite three times in a row because the line is only 10 minutes and you physically cannot make yourself leave.

And when you get home, you'll start planning the next one before you've finished unpacking.

## Stage 5: The Identity Shift

This is when "I like roller coasters" becomes "I'm a roller coaster enthusiast." It's subtle. It happens gradually. But one day you realize that coasters aren't just something you do. They're part of who you are.

You have a credit count and you know exactly what it is. You have a top 10 and you can rattle it off without thinking. You have opinions about manufacturers (B&M is consistent but sometimes plays it too safe, Intamin takes bigger swings but can be unreliable, RMC is on a different planet, Vekoma's new stuff is actually really good). You have a home park and you think of it the way sports fans think of their home team.

You follow industry news. You know which parks are getting new coasters next year. You've seen the construction photos. You've watched the testing videos. You have a shortlist of credits you need before you die.

Your non-enthusiast friends think you're a little weird. Your enthusiast friends think you're perfectly normal. You've found your people.

## Stage 6: The Thoosie

"Thoosie" comes from "enthusiast" with a kind of self-aware, tongue-in-cheek vibe. Not every enthusiast calls themselves a thoosie, but if you've made it this far in the pipeline, the shoe fits.

At this stage, you're deep. You might have a coaster podcast you listen to. You might attend events like CoasterMania or Thrills in the Hills. You might have friends you've never met in person but have been talking coasters with online for years. You might have a spreadsheet tracking every credit with dates, ratings, and notes.

You have strong opinions about things that would make a GP's eyes glaze over. Track gauges. Restraint types. The superiority of lapbar-only coasters. Whether trims on the first drop should be a criminal offense. The correct way to staple (you never staple, you ask them to leave it loose).

You can identify a coaster manufacturer by looking at the track profile. You know what a cobra roll is, what a pretzel loop is, what a Norwegian loop is. You've used the phrase "it has good pacing" about a roller coaster and meant it completely seriously.

And you still get butterflies at the top of the lift hill. That never goes away. Not at credit 50, not at credit 500.

## The Thing Nobody Tells You

The pipeline is one-way. Nobody goes backwards. Nobody rides Steel Vengeance and then decides they're done with coasters. Nobody hits 200 credits and says "that's enough." The hobby only deepens.

And that's the best part about it. There's always another park to visit. Always another credit to grab. Always another trip to plan. The community keeps growing, the manufacturers keep pushing boundaries, and every year there are new coasters that make you rearrange your entire top 10.

If you're reading this and you're somewhere in the early stages, welcome. If you're a full thoosie nodding along to every paragraph, you already know.

Either way, the next ride is always the one that matters most.
