For decades, Vekoma was the manufacturer enthusiasts loved to trash. Their SLCs rattled skulls and their boomerangs were clones stamped across every regional park on the planet. If you rode one, you rode them all, and your neck paid the price. Then something changed. Quietly, without much fanfare, Vekoma started building coasters that showed up on year-end best-of lists. The same company that gave the world Mind Eraser started delivering rides that enthusiasts would fly overseas to experience.

This is the story of how that happened.

## The Reputation Years

Vekoma's name became synonymous with two things in the 1990s and 2000s: clones and headbanging. The Suspended Looping Coaster, better known as the SLC, was the company's bread and butter. Parks could order one off a catalog, and Vekoma would ship it, bolt it together, and move on. Dozens of them went up around the world. Great Nor'Easter, Mind Eraser, T3, Arkham Asylum. Same layout, same rough ride, same headache.

The Boomerang was even more prolific. Over 50 installations worldwide, all nearly identical. A shuttle layout that pulled you through a cobra roll and a vertical loop, then did the whole thing backward. For parks on a budget, these were turnkey thrill rides. For enthusiasts, they were a punchline.

And it was not just the SLCs and Boomerangs. Vekoma's early attempts at bigger rides had issues too. Rock 'n' Roller Coaster at Hollywood Studios used Vekoma track (with a licensed Linear Synchronous Motor launch from another supplier), and while that ride worked well, many of their other installations struggled with maintenance-heavy trains and uncomfortable restraints. The over-the-shoulder harness on an SLC was basically a headbanging machine. Every transition slammed riders into the hard plastic ear guards.

The enthusiast community wrote Vekoma off. If a park announced a new Vekoma, the response was usually a groan.

## What Actually Changed

The turnaround did not happen overnight, and it was not one single decision. It was a ground-up rethinking of how Vekoma designed coasters.

**New train designs.** This was the biggest single factor. Vekoma developed new vest-style restraints that replaced the old over-the-shoulder harnesses. These vests sit against the chest and allow the head to move freely, eliminating the headbanging problem entirely. They also redesigned their wheel assemblies and bogies for smoother tracking through transitions.

**New track profiles.** Instead of the sharp, angular transitions that made SLCs rough, Vekoma started using computer-optimized heart-line rolls and smoother curve geometry. The difference is night and day. A modern Vekoma inversion flows through its arc instead of jerking you through it.

**Investment in R&D.** Vekoma opened a new engineering center and brought in fresh talent. They studied what B&M and Intamin were doing right and applied those principles to their own designs. They also started working with parks on custom layouts instead of just selling catalog models.

**Proving grounds.** Vekoma needed a park willing to take a chance on their new generation of rides. They found willing partners in European and Asian parks, where the Vekoma name did not carry the same baggage it did in the American enthusiast community.

## The New Generation

The proof started rolling in around 2017-2019.

**Lech Coaster** at Legendia in Poland (opened 2017) was one of the first rides to make people pay attention. A launched multi-inversion coaster with the new vest restraints, it rode like a completely different manufacturer built it. Smooth, intense, comfortable. Enthusiasts who traveled to Poland came back evangelizing it.

**Firestorm** at Gröna Lund in Stockholm hit hard. A compact but aggressive layout squeezed into the park's tiny footprint, with the kind of snappy inversions and sustained forces that would make B&M fans take notice.

**Formula 1 Coaster** at Ferrari World Abu Dhabi. A launched multi-LSM coaster with a top speed over 150 mph at one point during development (the production version runs around 150 kph). The scale alone showed that Vekoma was not just fixing their reputation, they were competing at the top level.

Then came the wave that sealed the deal.

**Ride to Happiness** at Plopsaland De Panne in Belgium (2021) is frequently cited as one of the best coasters in the world. A launched spinning coaster with five inversions, the ride delivers an experience so chaotic and rerideable that it regularly tops European polls. The spinning adds a dimension that no traditional layout can replicate, and the forces are relentless from launch to brakes.

**Voltron Nevera** at Europa-Park (2024) put Vekoma in the conversation for best new coaster of the year globally. A launched multi-pass coaster themed to the Rimac Nevera hypercar, with a beyond-vertical drop, multiple launches, and some of the most creative use of terrain and near-miss elements on any coaster anywhere.

## The Numbers Tell the Story

Look at the Golden Ticket Awards, Mitch Hawker polls, or any enthusiast ranking aggregate from 2015 versus 2024. Vekoma went from rarely appearing in top-50 lists to having multiple entries in top-25 lists worldwide. Ride to Happiness alone sits in the top 10 on several European rankings.

Parks that would never have considered Vekoma a decade ago are now placing orders. The company's order book has been consistently full, and new announcements keep landing. They have become a genuine alternative to B&M and Intamin for parks looking for a marquee attraction.

## What The Old Rides Tell Us

Here is the interesting part: the old SLCs and Boomerangs are still running. Dozens of them. Some have been retrofitted with new trains that help the roughness, but many are still operating with original hardware. Every time someone rides an old Mind Eraser and then tries a Ride to Happiness, the contrast makes the redemption arc even more dramatic.

Some parks have invested in Vekoma's retrofit program, swapping old SLC trains for the new vest-restraint models. The results are mixed. The trains help, but the track geometry is still the same angular profile from the 1990s. You can only polish a rough layout so much. The real transformation is in the new-build rides where both the track and the trains were designed together from scratch.

## Why It Matters

Vekoma's story matters because it proves that reputation in the coaster industry is not permanent. A manufacturer that listens, invests, and iterates can come back from the bottom. It also matters because more competition at the top means better rides for everyone. When B&M, Intamin, RMC, and Vekoma are all fighting for the same marquee contracts, parks benefit and riders benefit.

Ten years ago, a park announcing a new Vekoma would get sympathy from the enthusiast community. Today, it gets excitement. That is one of the more remarkable turnarounds in amusement industry history.
