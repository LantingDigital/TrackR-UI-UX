On March 13, 2026, the final piece of track was placed on Tormenta Rampaging Run at Six Flags Over Texas. The track is complete. Testing comes next. And when this ride opens later in 2026, it will simultaneously hold six world records and redefine what a B&M dive coaster can be.

Dive coasters have been part of the B&M catalog since Oblivion opened at Alton Towers in 1998. The concept is simple and effective: climb to the top, hold riders face-down over a near-vertical drop, release them, and let gravity do the work. The holding brake moment is one of the most memorable sensations in the industry. But dive coasters have always been mid-size rides. The tallest before Tormenta were Valravn and Yukon Striker, both at 223 feet. Good rides. Not giga-scale rides.

Tormenta changes that math completely.

## The Numbers

Every stat on this ride resets the dive coaster record book:

**Height: 309 feet.** The lift hill takes Tormenta into giga territory (300+ feet). No dive coaster has ever crossed that threshold. For reference, Fury 325 at Carowinds is 325 feet and Millennium Force at Cedar Point is 310 feet. Tormenta is playing in the same height class as the biggest coasters on the planet.

**First drop: 285 feet at 95 degrees beyond vertical.** Not just vertical. Beyond vertical. You are tilted past straight down as you fall. The holding brake at the top of this drop, suspending riders face-down 309 feet above the ground before releasing them into a 285-foot beyond-vertical plunge, is going to be one of the most intense moments on any coaster anywhere.

**Top speed: 87 mph.** The fastest dive coaster ever built. Yukon Striker maxed at 80 mph. Valravn at 75 mph. Tormenta adds another 7 mph on top of the previous record holder.

**Track length: 4,199 feet.** The longest dive coaster ever built by a significant margin. Yukon Striker was 3,625 feet. This is not an incremental improvement. Tormenta is over 15% longer than the previous record holder, which means more elements, more airtime, and more ride time.

**Vertical loop: 179 feet.** Not just the tallest loop on a dive coaster. The tallest vertical coaster loop in the world, period. The existing record was held by Full Throttle at Six Flags Magic Mountain at 160 feet. Tormenta's loop is taller by nearly 20 feet.

**Immelmann inversion: 218 feet.** The highest Immelmann inversion ever built on any coaster.

Six world records on a single installation. B&M does not typically chase records this aggressively. They are known for consistency and reliability, not for building the biggest thing possible. Tormenta represents a different gear for the Swiss manufacturer.

## The Layout

Based on the animated POV and construction photos, Tormenta's layout goes like this:

The train climbs the 309-foot lift hill and reaches the holding brake. The face-down hold over the 95-degree beyond-vertical drop is the signature moment. The train releases and plunges 285 feet into the first valley.

Out of the drop, the train enters the 218-foot Immelmann inversion, which takes riders up and over in a half-loop/half-roll combination at a height that is taller than most complete roller coasters.

The 179-foot vertical loop follows. This is an enormous element. Standing next to a 179-foot loop in person is going to be one of those moments where the scale is hard to process until you see a train going through it.

The layout continues with additional drops, airtime hills, and high-speed turns before the final brake run. The on-ride time from the drop to the final brakes is estimated around 50 seconds based on the animated POV, which is long for a dive coaster. Most dives feel like they end too quickly. Tormenta has the track length to sustain the experience.

B&M's floorless wide-row trains with vest restraints will be used, which is consistent with their modern dive coaster standard. The vest restraints allow head freedom during inversions (no headbanging) while keeping riders secure through the intense G-forces.

## The Themed Area: Rancho de la Tormenta

Tormenta is not just a coaster. It is the anchor of a new themed area called Rancho de la Tormenta, a Spanish village concept being built around the ride's footprint. The area includes a companion restaurant called Cocina Abuela, which adds a dining experience to the zone.

Six Flags has historically been light on theming compared to Disney and Universal. Rancho de la Tormenta represents a step toward more complete themed environments. Whether the execution matches the ambition remains to be seen, but the investment in a themed area rather than just a standalone coaster is a notable shift in approach for the chain.

## Construction Timeline

The build has been a multi-year project:

**June 2025:** B&M track and supports begin arriving, staged in the front preferred parking lot.

**Late September 2025:** Vertical construction starts with early station supports going up.

**Late 2025 to early 2026:** The lift hill rises, the vertical loop tops off, and track installation progresses through the layout.

**March 13, 2026:** Final piece of track placed, completing the circuit.

**Next steps:** Train delivery, mechanical completion, testing cycles, state inspection, and opening. Based on typical B&M timelines, testing could begin within weeks of track completion, with opening possible in late spring or summer 2026.

## Where It Fits in B&M's Dive Coaster History

B&M has built dive coasters for nearly three decades. Here is how the type has evolved:

| Ride | Park | Year | Height | Speed | Length |
|------|------|------|--------|-------|--------|
| Oblivion | Alton Towers | 1998 | ~180 ft | ~68 mph | ~1,200 ft |
| SheiKra | Busch Gardens Tampa | 2005 | 200 ft | 70 mph | 3,188 ft |
| Griffon | Busch Gardens Williamsburg | 2007 | 205 ft | 71 mph | 3,108 ft |
| Valravn | Cedar Point | 2016 | 223 ft | 75 mph | 3,415 ft |
| Yukon Striker | Canada's Wonderland | 2019 | 223 ft | 80 mph | 3,625 ft |
| Dr. Diabolical | Six Flags Fiesta Texas | 2022 | 150 ft | 60 mph | ~2,500 ft |
| **Tormenta** | **Six Flags Over Texas** | **2026** | **309 ft** | **87 mph** | **4,199 ft** |

The jump from Yukon Striker to Tormenta is the largest single-generation increase in the dive coaster model's history. It is not an incremental improvement. It is a fundamental scale change. Tormenta takes the dive concept from the "large coaster" category into the "giga" category, which is a leap that no one was expecting B&M to make with this ride type.

## The Enthusiast Reaction

The response on r/rollercoasters and across enthusiast forums has been overwhelmingly positive, but with a familiar caveat: dive coasters have always been considered "one-trick ponies" by a segment of the community. The holding brake drop is universally praised. The rest of the layout on most dive coasters is considered average.

Tormenta challenges that criticism directly. With 4,199 feet of track, a 179-foot loop, a 218-foot Immelmann, and additional elements that smaller dives simply do not have room for, Tormenta has the length and the element count to be a complete ride, not just a drop followed by filler.

The question that remains is how the ride feels. Numbers on paper do not always translate to ride quality. A 309-foot holding brake drop will be spectacular. But how do the forces feel through the loop? How does the pacing hold through 4,199 feet of track? Does the layout sustain intensity or does it fade after the initial drop sequence? Those answers come from riding it, and that day is getting close.

## What It Means for Six Flags Over Texas

Six Flags Over Texas has historically been a solid but not top-tier Six Flags park in terms of coaster lineup. It has good rides (New Texas Giant, Mr. Freeze, Titan) but has lacked a true destination coaster that would make out-of-state enthusiasts plan trips specifically for the park.

Tormenta changes that. A giga-scale, six-world-record dive coaster with a themed area is the kind of marquee attraction that puts a park on the map. Combined with the park's location in the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex (the fourth-largest metro area in the US), Tormenta gives Six Flags Over Texas a legitimate claim as a must-visit destination for coaster enthusiasts.

The investment also signals confidence from Six Flags corporate. You do not spend this kind of money on a park you are not committed to growing. Whatever the corporate restructuring and park sales mean for the broader chain, Six Flags Over Texas is clearly a priority property.
