# SoCal's Coaster Arms Race: Three Parks, Three New Rides, and an Olympics on the Horizon

**Six Flags Magic Mountain, Knott's Berry Farm, and Universal Studios Hollywood are all building new coasters at the same time. Here's why 2026 through 2028 is going to be wild for Southern California.**

---

Something interesting is happening in Southern California right now. Three major theme parks within driving distance of each other are all building new coasters simultaneously, and none of them are playing it safe. Six Flags Magic Mountain has mysterious blue track sitting on-site for what appears to be a world-first prototype. Knott's Berry Farm is finally rebuilding one of its most iconic rides after years of waiting. And Universal Studios Hollywood is putting up its first real outdoor thrill coaster. All of this is happening against the backdrop of the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics, which is about to flood the region with millions of visitors looking for things to do.

Welcome to SoCal's coaster arms race.

## Six Flags Magic Mountain: the 19th coaster nobody can officially name

Blue track pieces have arrived at Six Flags Magic Mountain. The park teased on social media that "the skyline won't be the same," but beyond that, the official details are thin. No name. No manufacturer confirmation. No opening date.

The unofficial details, though, are everywhere.

Screamscape, the veteran theme park rumor site, reports that the new ride is a Vekoma Thrill Glider, a concept that doesn't exist at any other park on Earth. Permit paperwork filed with the city reportedly labels it "Flags Thr Glider Coaster." Enthusiast forums like ParkFans and CoasterForce have been tracking the project under the working name "Cyber Cycle," describing it as an inverted, straddle-seat, motorbike-style launch coaster with inversions. Think somewhere between a TRON-style motorcycle ride and a traditional inverted coaster, but under the track instead of above it.

If that sounds like nothing you've ever ridden before, that's the point. Magic Mountain has always competed on coaster count (this would be number 19), but a world-first prototype from Vekoma would give them something no other park can claim. That's a different kind of flex than just adding another B&M to the skyline.

The timeline is fuzzy. Screamscape openly says they don't know whether the ride opens in late 2026 or slips to early 2027, and the park's own teaser leans toward a 2027 window. There's also been chatter that Six Flags corporate briefly wavered on the Thrill Glider concept entirely, considering a different Vekoma launched coaster before recommitting to the prototype. The project has been associated with clearing the Golden Bear Theater area and potentially replacing Ninja, the park's aging suspended coaster that has dealt with earthquake damage and deteriorating infrastructure. Multiple rumor trackers have advised enthusiasts to "ride Ninja now if you care," though no official closure has been announced.

## Knott's Berry Farm: MonteZOOMa finally comes home

Montezooma's Revenge was a Knott's icon. The Schwarzkopf shuttle loop operated from 1978 to 2019, launching riders from 0 to 55 mph in under four seconds through a massive vertical loop. When it closed, the park promised it would come back in a new form.

That promise has taken a while to deliver, but it's finally happening. MonteZOOMa: The Forbidden Fortress is a modernized launched shuttle with a new story, redesigned queue, and updated hardware. Track and supports have been going in throughout early 2026, with construction videos showing the rebuilt loop and launch area progressing. January and February updates revealed LSM (linear synchronous motor) infrastructure work, electrical installations, and a reconfigured entrance area.

MonteZOOMa isn't a brand-new footprint coaster. It's a legacy ride modernization. But in the context of the SoCal arms race, that's actually a smart play. Knott's doesn't need to outspend Magic Mountain or Universal on height and speed. It needs to give visitors a reason to come back, and a refreshed launch coaster built on genuine nostalgia does exactly that while keeping the park competitive on the "must-ride" list.

No firm opening date has been announced. Enthusiasts are hoping for 2026, and Screamscape's running log on the project suggests it's on track, but nobody is confident enough to call a specific month yet.

## Universal Studios Hollywood: the IP giant gets a real coaster

Here's the thing about Universal Studios Hollywood: it has never had a serious outdoor thrill coaster. The park has been built around studio tours, dark rides, and screen-based attractions. That changes this summer.

Fast & Furious: Hollywood Drift is Universal's first high-speed outdoor coaster at the Hollywood location, and the specs are not messing around. Official numbers put it at 72 mph with approximately 4,100 feet of elevated track, multiple inversions, and ride vehicles that spin 360 degrees to simulate high-speed drifting. The coaster threads over the park, including above the multi-level Starway escalators, with a themed garage-style queue matching the film franchise.

Universal is positioning Hollywood Drift as the fastest coaster in the entire Universal Destinations lineup. That's a statement aimed directly at the thrill-seeker demographic that Magic Mountain has owned for decades. And because it's tied to a massive IP, it gets marketing reach that a standalone coaster at a regional park can't touch.

A sister installation is also going into Universal Studios Florida, replacing the now-demolished Hollywood Rip Ride Rockit. But for the SoCal arms race, the Hollywood version is what matters: it puts Universal on the map as a legitimate coaster destination for the first time.

## The Olympics factor

All of this is happening in the shadow of the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics. A study by the Southern California Association of Governments estimates that the Games will generate between $13 and $18 billion in economic impact for the six-county region, with $8.9 to $12 billion concentrated in Los Angeles County alone. That's billions in visitor spending, media attention, and sponsor money flowing through the area.

The LA28 plan is explicitly "no-build" for permanent venues, meaning the Olympics themselves aren't funding new rides. But the tourism spike they'll create is enormous. Every major SoCal park has a financial incentive to have at least one significant new attraction open and marketable by the time those Games arrive. That lines up neatly with the current timeline: Universal targeting summer 2026 for Hollywood Drift, Knott's aiming MonteZOOMa for 2026, and Magic Mountain working toward a 2027 opening for its Vekoma prototype.

And then there's the corporate angle. The merged Six Flags Entertainment corporation now controls both Magic Mountain and Knott's Berry Farm. How they choose to stagger investments across those two parks, avoiding cannibalization while maximizing Olympics-era coverage, is one of the more interesting strategic questions in the industry right now. The LA Business Journal has noted that local parks have been expanding against this broader tourism backdrop, with the timing of marquee projects increasingly influenced by the approaching Games.

## What this means for thoosies

If you're planning a Southern California trip anytime between now and 2028, things are moving fast. By summer 2026, Universal will likely have Hollywood Drift open. Knott's should have MonteZOOMa running. And Magic Mountain's 19th coaster, whether it opens late 2026 or early 2027, will be something nobody has ever ridden anywhere.

Three parks. Three very different strategies. One region where they're all within an hour of each other, all competing for the same visitors, and all building at the same time. SoCal hasn't seen this kind of coaster investment overlap in years, and the Olympics are only going to turn up the pressure.

Keep your eyes on Southern California. This is going to be fun to watch.
