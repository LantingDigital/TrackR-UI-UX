# RMC Goes Small: How the Company That Built Steel Vengeance Ended Up at a Myrtle Beach Boardwalk Park

**Rocky Mountain Construction just announced their newest Raptor coaster. It's not at Six Flags. It's not at Cedar Fair. It's at a 60-year-old family park on Ocean Boulevard in Myrtle Beach. And that might be the smartest thing they've done in years.**

---

If you follow roller coasters at all, you know what RMC means. Rocky Mountain Construction is the company that turned aging wooden coasters into some of the most celebrated rides on the planet. New Texas Giant. Twisted Colossus. Steel Vengeance. Iron Gwazi. For the better part of a decade, RMC was the most exciting name in the industry, and their projects were almost exclusively at major chain parks with major chain budgets.

That's changing. And the clearest sign of it is a 100-foot single-rail coaster currently being built at Family Kingdom Amusement Park, a modest seaside park on Ocean Boulevard in Myrtle Beach, South Carolina.

## The announcement nobody expected

At the 2025 IAAPA Expo in Orlando, the biggest trade show in the attractions industry, RMC made an announcement that genuinely surprised people. They were building a custom Raptor coaster for Family Kingdom, a park most enthusiasts knew only as "the place with Swamp Fox." Not a Six Flags record-breaker. Not another woodie conversion. A ground-up build at a boardwalk park in South Carolina.

Dean Corl of Family Kingdom spoke with Funworld at the IAAPA booth about the partnership. The message was simple: a modern thrill coaster is coming to a classic park. But the subtext was bigger than one ride. RMC, the company that built 200-foot hybrids for the largest chains in the world, was going to a boardwalk park in South Carolina. That says something about where the company is headed.

Coaster101 called it one of the most surprising reveals at the entire expo. CoasterForce set up a dedicated tracking thread immediately. Reddit lit up with speculation. Everyone wanted to know the same thing: why Family Kingdom?

## What they're building

Details came out in a February 2026 Coaster101 feature based on an ACE (American Coaster Enthusiasts) visit and park presentation. Here's what we know:

The Family Kingdom Raptor will stand 100 feet tall, about 30 feet taller than Swamp Fox, the park's beloved wooden coaster. It will hit 52 mph across 1,920 feet of single-rail track with 3 inversions and 5 airtime moments. The layout is described as very similar to Fire Runner at Lost Island Theme Park in Iowa, with an extra airtime hill at the end and the final brake run flipped so the train turns left into the station instead of right.

The ride uses RMC's single-rail Raptor track with inline single-seat cars, one rider per row. If you've never ridden a Raptor, the experience is distinct from any other coaster type. Your legs dangle. The rail is directly beneath you. There's nothing next to you. Every inversion, every airtime moment, every drop happens with nothing but air between you and the ground.

The name hasn't been announced yet. The only hint from the park: "We're near the ocean, use your imagination." Track shipments started in early March 2026, with Family Kingdom announcing "the first of 28 deliveries" on their social media. The target opening is late summer 2026, with Coaster101 suggesting to expect it closer to Labor Day than Memorial Day.

When it opens, it will be the Southeast's first RMC single-rail Raptor, Family Kingdom's fourth coaster, and a signature addition for the park's 60th anniversary.

## What is a Raptor, anyway?

RMC introduced the single-rail Raptor concept at IAAPA 2015 alongside its bigger sibling, the T-Rex. Where T-Rex was designed for massive, multi-train layouts that could compete with traditional steel hypercoasters, the Raptor was smaller on purpose. Compact footprint, lower cost. The idea was a ride system that smaller regional and independent parks could actually afford while still getting the RMC name and the RMC ride experience.

The first two opened in 2018: Wonder Woman Golden Lasso Coaster at Six Flags Fiesta Texas and RailBlazer at California's Great America. Both used the same compact layout with 3 inversions. They were intense and disorienting, and riders loved them immediately. Enthusiasts ranked them among the best coasters at their respective parks despite being shorter and less visually imposing than the big B&M and Intamin rides around them.

Then the line evolved. Jersey Devil Coaster at Six Flags Great Adventure (2021) and Wonder Woman Flight of Courage at Six Flags Magic Mountain (2022) used longer, custom "extended" layouts. Stunt Pilot at Silverwood (2021) put a customized Raptor at RMC's home park, a mid-size independent in northern Idaho. Fire Runner at Lost Island (2025) went to a very small standalone theme park in Waterloo, Iowa. And YoY at Walibi Holland in the Netherlands pushed the model's limits with 6 inversions on its thrill side.

Enthusiasts generally split the Raptor line into three categories: the original compact layouts (RailBlazer, Golden Lasso), the extended custom layouts (Jersey Devil, Flight of Courage, Fire Runner, Family Kingdom), and the experimental configurations (YoY). The trend line is clear. With each generation, the Raptor has moved further from the big chains and closer to independent parks.

## Why this matters: RMC's business problem

To understand why RMC is building at Family Kingdom, you have to understand the company's business model and its vulnerabilities.

RMC was founded in 2001 by Fred Grubb as a ride construction and wooden coaster repair company. The breakthrough came in 2011 with New Texas Giant at Six Flags Over Texas, the first Iron Horse (I-Box) conversion that took a rough, aging wooden coaster and transformed it into a steel-tracked hybrid that rode like nothing else in the industry. That concept was RMC's goldmine. Parks had old wooden coasters they wanted to refresh. RMC could take the existing structure, re-track it with their proprietary steel rail, and deliver what was effectively a new coaster for less than the cost of building from scratch.

Through the 2010s, the conversions kept coming. Iron Rattler. Twisted Cyclone. Wicked Cyclone. Steel Vengeance. Iron Gwazi. Each one landed at a major chain park. Cedar Fair. Six Flags. SeaWorld/Busch. The reviews were spectacular and the awards piled up. But the business had a structural limitation: there are only so many old wooden coasters worth converting, and they're mostly owned by two companies.

A YouTube analysis titled "The Rising Uncertainty of Rocky Mountain Construction" made this point explicitly. RMC's most successful product line was heavily dependent on large parks recycling aging wooden coasters into marketable "new" rides. When the supply of viable candidates thins out, or when the chains' CapEx cycles shift, the pipeline shrinks. The Six Flags and Cedar Fair merger into one company only intensified this concern. One corporate entity now controls most of RMC's historical client base, with portfolio optimization and potential park divestitures adding uncertainty.

## The Raptor solution

This is where the Raptor line becomes strategically critical for RMC's future.

Raptors are not conversions. They're ground-up builds on RMC's proprietary single-rail system. They don't need an existing wooden coaster to start from. They don't need a 200-foot plot of cleared land. And they don't need a Cedar Fair or Six Flags budget to be financially viable.

Industry analysts and enthusiast commentators consistently cite the same advantages. The single-rail track and reduced support steel keep capital and site costs down. The compact footprint fits land-constrained sites like seaside boardwalks and older midways. Single-row trains can dispatch efficiently for decent throughput without massive stations. And despite being smaller on paper, Raptors deliver high perceived intensity and a unique visual profile that lets parks market a "world-class RMC" without needing 200-foot stats.

And Raptors are modular. RMC can reuse core design elements (drop, dive loop, cutback, stall, inline twist, airtime hill) and rearrange them to fit different terrains and site constraints. The result is a semi-standardized product that can be sold repeatedly with minor layout tweaks. That's very different from a one-off Steel Vengeance or Iron Gwazi, and it's much closer to a sustainable business model.

The trajectory tells the story. Stunt Pilot at Silverwood proved a Raptor could work at a mid-size independent. ArieForce One (an I-Box, not a Raptor, but a ground-up build) at Fun Spot America Atlanta showed RMC would work with a tiny chain known for go-karts and flat rides. Fire Runner at Lost Island went to a very small standalone park in Iowa. And now Family Kingdom in Myrtle Beach. Each step moves further from the flagship chain model and deeper into the independent park world.

## What this means for the industry

Family Kingdom getting an RMC Raptor matters beyond just one park's coaster count.

One of the most respected coaster manufacturers on the planet is now building for parks that historically would never have been realistic clients. A decade ago, you couldn't have imagined RMC hardware at a Myrtle Beach boardwalk park. Now it's under construction. If this works, if the ride delivers, if it drives attendance and revenue for a 60-year-old independent park, it opens the door for other small and mid-size parks to pursue the same thing.

The Raptor model gives independent parks access to the kind of ride experience and brand recognition that was previously locked behind corporate CapEx budgets. Parks get world-class rides. Riders get RMC experiences in places they'd never expect. And RMC gets a path forward in a post-merger, post-conversion landscape where the big chain contracts might not flow as freely as they did in the 2010s.

For enthusiasts, it means the RMC experience is spreading to places you wouldn't normally plan a trip around. Myrtle Beach is a vacation destination. Families go there for the beach. Now there's an RMC Raptor on the boardwalk. That's how a niche manufacturer becomes a mainstream name, and it's how a 60-year-old family park stays relevant for the next 60.
