# Tormenta Rampaging Run: Six Flags Just Built the World's First Giga Dive Coaster

**Six world records attached to one ride. 309 feet of B&M steel in the Texas sun. And it's not even open yet.**

---

There are about eighteen dive coasters on the planet. They all share the same DNA: wide trains, a slow climb, a few seconds of staring straight down at the ground, and then a near-vertical drop that makes your stomach relocate. The model has been a crowd-pleaser since Oblivion debuted at Alton Towers back in 1998, but enthusiasts have spent two decades calling them one-trick ponies. Big drop, a couple inversions, brakes. Done.

Tormenta Rampaging Run is about to change that conversation.

Six Flags Over Texas is building the first-ever giga dive coaster, and the numbers are so far beyond anything the dive model has produced that it barely feels like the same ride type. At 309 feet tall, it clears the previous record holders by 86 feet. That's not an upgrade. That's a different category entirely.

## The six records

When Tormenta opens, it will hold six world records simultaneously:

1. **Tallest dive coaster** at 309 feet
2. **Fastest dive coaster** at 87 mph
3. **Longest dive coaster** at 4,199 feet of track
4. **Tallest vertical loop on any coaster** at 179 feet
5. **Highest Immelmann inversion** at 218 feet
6. **Highest beyond-vertical drop** plunging 285 feet at 95 degrees

That vertical loop alone is taller than most complete roller coasters. The Immelmann sits higher than the lift hill on Valravn, which held six dive records of its own when it opened at Cedar Point in 2016. Tormenta doesn't just break these records. It leaves them behind.

## What came before

To understand why Tormenta matters, you need to know what B&M has done with the dive model over the last 28 years.

Oblivion started the whole thing in 1998 at Alton Towers. It was a proof of concept: a wide train, a vertical drop into a tunnel. Simple, terrifying, and wildly popular. SheiKra at Busch Gardens Tampa added inversions in 2005. Griffon at Busch Gardens Williamsburg went floorless in 2007. Each generation pushed the model a little further.

Then came the heavyweights. Valravn opened at Cedar Point in 2016 at 223 feet with six world records. Yukon Striker at Canada's Wonderland answered with 223 feet and 80 mph, claiming the longest and fastest dive titles. Dr. Diabolical's Cliffhanger brought the 95-degree beyond-vertical angle to Six Flags Fiesta Texas in a more compact package.

But none of them cracked 300 feet. The dive coaster model topped out in the 220s while B&M's hyper and giga coasters like Fury 325 and Leviathan kept climbing. The dive line felt like it had a ceiling.

Tormenta blows through that ceiling. At 309 feet, it's a full giga coaster that happens to hold you face-down over a beyond-vertical drop before letting go. The energy coming off that first drop is enormous, and B&M is using all of it: 87 mph through a 179-foot loop, a 218-foot Immelmann, additional drops and high-speed turns across 4,199 feet of total track. The animated POV suggests around 50 seconds from the drop to the final brakes. For a dive coaster, that's an eternity.

## How it got built

Track started arriving at Six Flags Over Texas in June 2025, staged in the front parking lot. Vertical construction kicked off in late September, and on September 25, Six Flags officially announced Tormenta Rampaging Run along with the name, theme, all six records, and the 2026 opening target.

From there, the milestones came fast. First track pieces went up in early October. By late January 2026, the record-breaking Immelmann inversion was complete, a milestone that People magazine covered as a major construction event. On February 6, the city of Arlington issued a press release celebrating the completion of the 179-foot vertical loop, calling it the tallest vertical coaster loop ever built. Blooloop reported around the same time that construction of the dive coaster structure was complete.

Six Flags opened its 65th season on February 28, 2026, but Tormenta wasn't ready for opening day. The ride is currently in the testing and commissioning phase. Enthusiast speculation based on construction progress and historical B&M testing timelines points to a late spring or summer 2026 debut, though Six Flags has not confirmed a specific date. Every official communication says the same thing: Tormenta will open "later this year" as the centerpiece of the anniversary season.

## Rancho de la Tormenta

Six Flags went deeper on theming for this one than they have on maybe any project in recent memory. Tormenta sits in a completely reimagined area called Rancho de la Tormenta, themed around a secluded Spanish village and the Running of the Bulls.

The backstory: the village was originally named for the storms that swept the plains, but it became famous for a legendary bull born there with unmatched strength and speed. Matadors from across Spain came to challenge him and failed. The town was renamed in his honor.

Guests enter under colorful banners with jubilant Spanish music playing overhead, with the 309-foot coaster towering in the background. The trains are styled after the vibrant costumes of bull run participants. The entire plaza, queue, and dining area are integrated into one cohesive experience, which is not something you hear about a Six Flags addition every day.

## Cocina Abuela

The new restaurant anchoring Rancho de la Tormenta is Cocina Abuela (also referenced as Cocina de la Abuela in city press materials), a scratch-kitchen dining spot themed to Spanish and Latin American home cooking. The menu includes Spanish rice, patatas bravas, chicken tinga, and fresh house-made guacamole. Park and media descriptions emphasize bold flavors and scratch-made quality over typical theme park quick-serve fare.

The restaurant sits right at the entrance to the plaza, and its design and aromas are deliberately used as part of the land's story introduction. For a Six Flags park, this level of food-integrated theming is a real statement about the direction of the post-merger Six Flags Entertainment company.

## What enthusiasts are saying

The coaster community is paying very close attention. Facebook groups and Reddit threads have been tracking every construction photo, every piece of track, and every train color reveal since summer 2025. Multiple YouTube channels have produced analysis videos, animated POVs, and timeline speculation content.

Most enthusiasts are flat-out excited. The scale is unprecedented for a dive coaster, and the combination of giga height with massive inversions is something nobody has experienced before. It reads as Six Flags' boldest modern investment and a signal that the merged Six Flags Entertainment corporation is willing to spend on flagship attractions.

There are skeptics too. One Reddit thread called it "the world's least interesting giga coaster," arguing that the long transitions between elements and the use of vest restraints (which limit airtime sensation compared to lap bars) could hold it back from the sustained intensity that enthusiasts expect from a true giga. The debate is real, and it won't be settled until people actually ride it.

But skeptics or not, nobody is ignoring it. Tormenta is the biggest new dive coaster opening anywhere in 2026, and when testing footage starts appearing this spring, the conversation is only going to get louder.
